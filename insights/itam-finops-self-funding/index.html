ITAM and FinOps Programmes That Fund Themselves

Most ITAM and FinOps programmes lose credibility because they treat cost avoidance as a saving. Here's how to structure a self-funding model that recycles real savings into the next phase — without asking finance for another pound.

BM Bimal Mohanan · May 2026 · ~10 min read

I've spent enough time on both sides of the managed service relationship to recognise the pattern. The pitch rarely changes: commit the budget, trust the methodology, and the transformation will follow. The capability you're paying for doesn't exist yet, but the slides are excellent.

What follows is predictable. You spend the next several quarters defending an investment that hasn't yet produced anything you can point a CFO at, while the programme runs in permanent survival mode. I've watched this happen across multiple organisations, and the cause is almost never the ambition or the methodology. It's the economics.

When you position ITAM or FinOps as a cost centre that needs continuous budget allocation, you're working against the grain of how organisations actually behave. Finance resists ongoing spend on capabilities whose value isn't immediately legible. That resistance is rational, and no amount of conviction in the approach overcomes it.

So the question worth asking is a different one. Can these programmes fund their own evolution out of the savings they generate — real money that was leaving the organisation and now isn't, recycled into the next phase without another approval cycle? In my experience, they can, provided you're disciplined about what counts as a saving and how you structure the arrangement before any work begins.

Here is how that works in practice.

Cost avoidance is not a saving, and finance knows it

Most ITAM and FinOps programmes lose credibility with finance teams for one reason: they present cost avoidance as if it were the same thing as a saving.

“We could have spent this, but now we won't” holds up right until someone in finance asks the obvious questions. Was this budgeted? Was it contracted? Or was it spend that was never going to happen anyway? Cost avoidance doesn't free up capital, reduce a budget line, or release money for reallocation. It's a useful number for a report and very little else.

When I talk about a self-funding model, I mean direct cost savings only: a hard reduction in expenditure that was contracted, budgeted, and actively flowing out of the business. Money you can track precisely, account for in real budget terms, and demonstrably reinvest without going back for fresh approval. That distinction shapes everything downstream — the stakeholder agreements, the measurement framework, and the reinvestment mechanics.

The 20% reinvestment model

The model rests on a straightforward principle. When you identify and actually transact a genuine cost reduction, 20% flows back into the programme. The other 80% returns to the business.

That percentage is deliberate. It sits below the threshold where finance teams start to push back on reinvestment, but it's substantial enough to fund meaningful capability. Finance gets immediate relief on four-fifths of the saving. The programme gets sustained, self-generated funding from the remaining fifth.

The critical move is to agree this structure before any analysis starts — not after you've delivered value and find yourself negotiating from a weaker position. Before examining a single contract, you put budget reality on the table. You ingest actual spend data, run it against usage telemetry, and set savings targets based on what the data shows rather than what you'd like it to show. You quantify what is genuinely transactable given the contractual and operational constraints, not what looks impressive on a chart.

When stakeholders understand the success criteria up front, the 20% is baked into the original agreement. It stops being a point of contention later.

Getting organisations to show you the truth

The biggest source of friction in this work isn't technical. It's persuading an organisation to be honest about how it actually spends.

There's real institutional discomfort here — embarrassment about waste, uncertainty about whether the asset inventory is accurate, and a protective instinct around budget data that might expose past decisions. People are understandably reluctant to surface the shadow IT, the contracts nobody fully understands, the licence sprawl everyone suspects, and the vendor relationships where they sense they're being over-extracted but can't prove it.

The way through is to make their success the objective. Not to position yourself as the rescuer, and not to extract maximum commercial value. The aim is to make them look credible to their CFO, their CIO, and their procurement leadership. When people believe you're genuinely there to improve their standing rather than demonstrate your own cleverness, the defensiveness drops and they start telling you where the problems actually are.

Most “savings” can't actually be transacted

Here's a failure mode that quietly destroys credibility: identifying savings that cannot be converted into budget relief.

I've seen licence position analyses that reveal significant over-licensing. The client gets excited, leadership starts planning how to redeploy the capital, and then the contractual reality lands — they're locked into a multi-year enterprise agreement with committed spend that can't be reduced mid-term. You may have spotted £500,000 in unused licences, but if you're obligated to keep paying for another 24 months, that isn't a saving you can transact today. It's intelligence for the next renewal.

This is why the contract analysis has to come before any savings commitment. You examine the terms, the licence use rights, the exit clauses, and the renewal windows, and you separate what can be executed now from what's contractually trapped until a specific window opens. The FinOps Foundation has noted that organisations have absorbed substantial audit penalties precisely because they lacked visibility into unused or poorly managed licences. This discipline is what separates a programme that delivers real budget relief from one that produces good-looking reports and nothing else.

When the savings aren't where you expected

When the savings don't materialise where you first projected, that isn't a failure — it's a normal part of discovery, provided you can move quickly to the next opportunity.

The analytical platform we use, Acuity, lets us test multiple hypotheses in parallel. If one area proves less promising under scrutiny, we move to the next candidate without losing momentum, which keeps stakeholder confidence intact during a phase that would otherwise feel uncertain.

The aggregate picture confirms the opportunity is real and large. Zylo's State of SaaS Management Index puts the average enterprise at roughly $19.8 million a year wasted on unused SaaS licences, with large enterprises (10,000+ employees) carrying total SaaS spend of $123M–$375M and analyst estimates putting around 25% of it as waste. The question was never whether the waste exists. It's whether you can locate it, quantify it precisely, and transact it within the contractual constraints you actually face.

The first win sets the trajectory

The whole programme depends on demonstrating capability quickly in the first phase. The fastest we've gone from initial data ingestion to a validated, presentable result was 72 hours: a 12% cloud cost reduction, transactability confirmed, presented to stakeholders while the engagement still had energy behind it.

That early win does three things. It validates the analytical approach, it builds trust through demonstrated competence rather than promises, and it creates the momentum needed to secure buy-in for the longer journey. From there the model compounds — the first win funds the second phase, the second funds the third, and the third builds the internal capability that sustains everything afterwards without external justification. Scope expands organically: more spend categories, a broader analytical view, the next priorities identified during discovery. The programme grows on the back of the value it generates.

Why self-funding programmes stall, and how to prevent it

I've seen self-funding programmes lose momentum after the first or second phase despite delivering genuine savings. The cause is consistent: no coherent roadmap beyond the immediate win, and no real stakeholder commitment to the multi-phase journey.

What determines whether a programme compounds or fades is how clearly you've defined success — not vague aspirations about maturity, but concrete outcomes tied to how each stakeholder is actually evaluated. The ITAM manager is usually measured on asset visibility and compliance. Finance is measured on cost reduction. Procurement is measured on contract savings. If you structure the programme to deliver measurable progress against all three, with live monthly reporting each of them can take upwards, then everyone sees it as instrumental to their own success. At that point it stops needing defensive justification.

You will hit moments where priorities conflict — a compliance improvement that temporarily raises costs, against a procurement lead measured on quarterly savings. The resolution is transparency about the trade-off. If everyone bought into the long-term journey at the outset, they'll show flexibility when you put the sequencing and the expected payoff timeline clearly in front of them. That transparency is what lets a programme survive leadership changes and budget pressure.

Why converging ITAM and FinOps multiplies the value

There's a structural reason to converge these two disciplines rather than run them separately. Gartner has projected that by 2026, the majority of enterprises with mature cloud strategies will unify their Software Asset Management and FinOps capabilities into a single central function — and that organisations integrating ITAM and FinOps practices to manage SaaS and cloud-based AI will reduce their AI expenses by 30% through 2028.

ITAM gives you visibility into on-premises infrastructure and traditional software licensing. FinOps gives you visibility into cloud consumption and dynamic resource optimisation. Together they cover ground that neither sees in isolation. The self-funding model works precisely because you aren't confined to one domain — when licence optimisation hits a contractual wall, you pivot to cloud cost reduction; when cloud savings plateau, you move to vendor renegotiation. That breadth is what gives the programme its durability.

What you're actually building

You're not buying a cost-reduction service. You're building institutional capability.

The self-funding structure turns ITAM and FinOps from time-bound projects into capabilities that compound across cycles. Each phase builds technical expertise, sharpens the analytical process, strengthens stakeholder relationships, and widens the scope of what can be governed effectively. The 20% reinvestment funds organisational learning and capability transfer — the institutional muscle memory that keeps the discipline alive long after the formal engagement ends.

Rather than creating a permanent dependency on external expertise, you're building internal capability that becomes genuinely self-sustaining. The programme doesn't need ongoing justification because it visibly generates more value than it consumes, in hard budget terms finance can see.

The organisations that understand this don't just reduce IT spend. They build an advantage through better asset stewardship and sharper resource allocation, free up capital for strategic investment instead of letting it leak through inefficiency, and reposition their IT teams as value generators rather than cost centres under perpetual scrutiny.

And they do it without asking finance for another pound.
