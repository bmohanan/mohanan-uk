Leadership lessons

Forged in the trenches.

My leadership philosophy was shaped by watching both exceptional and deeply flawed managers in action. Here are the lessons I carry forward — the good ones, and the ones learned the hard way.

From great managers
  • Spot potential early. The best leaders see talent and drive before people see it in themselves. They invest, challenge, and give stretch opportunities. I now make it a priority to identify and nurture hidden potential in my teams.
  • Build confidence through trust. They gave autonomy and backed me up, even when I stumbled. Psychological safety accelerates growth far more than micromanagement ever will.
From difficult managers
  • Clarity and respect are non-negotiable. Ambiguity, favouritism or public criticism destroys morale. I refuse to lead through fear or ego — my standard is radical candour, delivered with respect.
  • Own your mistakes. Leaders who couldn't admit fault or deflected blame taught me the power of accountability. I create environments where people feel safe to own outcomes.
The standard I set

I lead by combining high expectations with genuine belief in people. My goal is to be the kind of manager who accelerates careers, not one whom people endure. Every team member should leave stronger, clearer about their strengths, and more confident than when they joined.

Seeing potential + rejecting toxic patterns — that’s how I define effective leadership.

Deep dive

Psychological safety.

The foundation of every high-performing team I’ve been part of — and the silent killer of those that struggled. It’s the shared belief that you can take risks, admit mistakes, ask questions, and voice ideas without fear of embarrassment, punishment, or rejection. When it exists, people bring their full selves and best thinking. When it’s absent, they play it safe, hide problems, and innovation dies.

What great managers taught me
  • Created environments where failing forward was expected, not punished.
  • Responded to mistakes with curiosity instead of criticism.
  • Admitted their own errors openly, modelling vulnerability at the top.
  • Celebrated smart risks, even when outcomes weren’t perfect.
What difficult managers showed me
  • Public call-outs, blame-shifting and inconsistent standards destroy trust instantly.
  • When people feel judged rather than supported, they stop speaking up — even when they see major risks.
  • Ego-driven leadership makes teams performative instead of productive.
My commitment as a leader
  • Ask for feedback regularly and respond to it without defensiveness.
  • Frame mistakes as learning opportunities — “what did we learn?”, not “who messed up?”
  • Encourage dissent and reward people who challenge the status quo constructively.
  • Be transparent about my own uncertainties and decisions.

Psychological safety isn’t softness — it’s the multiplier that turns talented individuals into an unstoppable team.

I read the board. I choose this on purpose.

My goal is to create the kind of environment where people feel safe enough to be bold.
